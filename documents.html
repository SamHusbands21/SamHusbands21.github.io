Differences in performance between logistic regression and machine learning for clinical risk prediction

Supervised machine learning techniques such as random forests, support vector machines and feedforward neural networks are often used in the context of clinical risk prediction. Despite their theoretical benefits over logistic regression in being able to model non-linearities and interactions automatically, the performance of these algorithms is unclear in recent studies. This thesis finds that some supervised machine learning algorithms confer a performance advantage in discrimination when predicting mortality over a ten-year period, although this often comes at the cost of considerable computational expense and poorer calibration. The benefits are most obvious in the cases of random forest and ensemble learning, and the performance improvement is strongest where sample size and feature count are high.

Does Marriage Cause Higher Wages for Men? Does Religion Affect the Returns to Marriage?

This paper discusses the effect of marriage on male wages using contemporary British panel data (Understanding Society, 2009–2018). Marriage theoretically increases male wages through household specialisation and signalling effects. Initially using pooled OLS and then a difference-in-differences estimation to address selection bias, this paper finds a statistically significant causal increase in male wages of approximately 6.5% following marriage. The apparent larger premium for non-religious men observed in raw data disappears once fixed effects control for selection in time-invariant unobservable characteristics, suggesting the differential is driven by selection rather than genuinely higher returns.

🎓 University of Warwick 📅 April 2019 📚 BSc Economics
